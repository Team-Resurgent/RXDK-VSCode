Audio Overview

This article is intended to cover a brief introduction to some of the challenges of audio design for video games and to introduce you to some of the features (and possibilities) available with Xbox.

Audio Design

Sound design for video games is often compared with sound design for motion pictures. The major elements for both games and motion pictures include:

  • Music
  • Sound effects
  • Dialog
  • Ambience

However, the comparison with movies is somewhat misleading. While you still need to create high-quality music and sound effects, sound designers and composers for video games need to make them flexible enough to change as the action on the screen changes. For example, with a movie, the composer and sound designer can get a good idea of what is going to happen next scene by looking at the script or the actual footage of the movie. With a game (unless you don't give the player any choices), you have no idea what is going to happen next. Will the player open the door on the right to an empty room or open the door on the left to a room full of enemies?

A further challenge is creative. Because games are generally played for many hours over many sessions, repetitive background music can become tiresome and sound effects can become overly predictable. This unnatural repetition also has the unfortunate effect of disrupting the mood of the game as the player becomes annoyed at the soundtrack.

What sound designers and composers for games need is the ability to do effects and mixing in real time as the game is being played. The Xbox is a big step in the right direction.

Audio Features

A lot of the features provided with Xbox give you a lot of flexibility to create a great audio experience for your title. For example, here is a rough list of what features the audio hardware provides:

  • Dolby® Digital and Dolby® Surround encoding
  • ADPCM decoding
  • Sound effects
  • Filters
  • Low frequency oscillators
  • Envelopes
  • Mixing
  • Storage options (DVD, hard disk, memory)

Outside of a few CPU cycles for accessing data from the hard disk or DVD, most of the audio work is being take care of by the Xbox Media Communications Processor (MCP), leaving the CPU free to handle other things that need to get done.

In addition to the features provided by the audio hardware, the Xbox also has a feature that allows users to rip tracks from their favorite CDs and create playlists. The tracks are stored on a special section of the hard disk, which titles can access using functions provided in the Xbox Title Libraries.

For an overview of using the playlists and soundtrack support, see the Soundtrack Support on Xbox section.

Benefits

So what good does all of this do your title? Here are a few ideas:

  • Create your sound effects on the Xbox. Suppose you're doing a game in a prehistoric setting and you've got a lot of effects based on an elephant sound. You would normally do one set of effects to make it sound like a mammoth, another set of effects to make it sound like a T. rex, and so on. Under normal circumstances, you'd probably have to save all of those effects as separate wave files. Instead, you could just save the original wave (with the elephant sound) and do the effects on the Xbox MCP to make all of the other variations you need.
  • Dynamically alter your content to respond to changes in the game. In the past, you may have needed to author separate content for each time the situation in the game changed. For example, you might have one wave with a slow, steady heartbeat for when the player's character is healthy and another wave with a fast, frantic heartbeat for when the character is low on life. With Xbox, you could use the same wave data and alter a few things (tempo, pitch, and so on) to get the desired effect.
  • Use the 3D sound features. Instead of recording a separate sound for when something is behind a wall or just moving to different positions around the listener, use the 3D positioning and I3DL2 features of DirectSound and DirectMusic to do it for you.
  • Take advantage of the user's stereo system. While movies have been able to take advantage of Dolby Digital and Dolby® Pro Logic® stereo systems for a while now, games haven't really be able to do as much. With Xbox, your title has access to Dolby Digital and Dolby Surround encoding on the hardware, so your title could sound as good as a movie on DVD.
  • Enable users to listen to their own music in the background. While you can't depend on users ripping their CDs or creating their own playlists, allowing them to listen to their own music during the game could enhance the player's enjoyment of the experience. Imagine being able to listen to your favorite driving tune while driving cross country instead of listening to synthesized radio music in the background.

While this list is far from comprehensive, hopefully it gives you an idea of at least a few things you can take advantage of in your title.
