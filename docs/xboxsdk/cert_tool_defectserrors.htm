Dealing with Media Defects and Errors

 

This white paper describes the behavior of the Xbox® video game system from Microsoft® when media defects are present on an Xbox Game Disc, as well as recommendations for minimizing the impacts of these errors within your game.

 

The Issue

 

Disc media in any consumer electronics device is often subject to a wide variety of real-world handling issues that produce defects not present on a pristine manufactured disc. Fingerprints, smudges, scratches, and worse can all reduce the readability of data on the media. On the Xbox, this can be exacerbated both by the dual-layer nature of the Xbox Game Disc and by the high speed of the drive—especially on the outer edge.

 

The Xbox, through both hardware and software, attempts to compensate for media defects (described in detail below). It is important to understand how this affects your game and what you can do to compensate.

 

System Error Handling

 

The Xbox contains a constant angular velocity (CAV) disc drive. In this type of a drive, data is read at the highest rate on the outside edge, and the speed decreases linearly as you move toward the inner circumference. On the Xbox, this speed will range from approximately 3 MB/sec on the interior of the disc to 6 MB/sec on the outer edge.

Because of this, it makes a lot of sense to place data on the outer edge of the disc in order to get the highest transfer rate. However, because of the higher transfer rate, this also means that the system has the least amount of time to react to and correct errors. Therefore, this area of the drive is the most likely to be susceptible to media errors.

 

Internally, the driver has three settings for spindle speed: minimum, maximum, and medium. The default spindle speed is always maximum. In the case of certain media defects, the drive returns various errors that cause the IDE driver to slow the spindle speed down (first to medium, and then to minimum speed). Once the speed is lowered, a certain number of sectors must be successfully read before the driver advances the spindle speed. If the drive is operating at minimum speed, a read of 4,096 sectors is required. At medium speed, 8,192 sectors are required. If the drive is ratcheted down to minimum spindle speed, it must travel through medium speed before returning to maximum speed. At minimum speed, the throughput is approximately 33 percent of maximum spindle speed. Medium speed is approximately 66 percent of maximum speed.

 

Testing Your Title

 

Although defects (especially those related to handling) are a normal aspect of optical media such as CDs, DVDs, and Xbox Game Discs, the errors can result in degradation of performance. The speed can be reduced up to 66 percent in the case of a severe enough scratch or fingerprint. While it is not required that your title gracefully handle such a drop in throughput transparently to the user, it is important to ensure that the game is able to recover and does not hang or crash the system. Unfortunately, the current version of the Xbox DVD Emulator does not allow for robust fault injection (this will be addressed in a future release). There are some steps to take, however, to test your game under more adverse conditions:

 

  • Move away from using the Xbox hard disk as a test platform as soon in the development process as possible. If you wait until near-final bits before testing on real media, it is much more likely that you will have difficulty addressing these types of performance issues. The hard disk is much faster than the disc drive even in the best of circumstances.

  • Use the Xbox DVD Emulator or burn DVD-R media to perform more realistic tests. CD-RW is a bad choice. It has much different characteristics than DVD media and does not work well with the Xbox disc drive. The DVD emulator, however, once set up, is a simple interim development tool that can achieve real-world performance while in game. DVD-R is a good backup, although you do not have the same ability to carefully lay out files that you do on Xbox Game Discs, and the media is not dual-layer. It should also be noted that DVD-R media do not have the same performance characteristics as an Xbox Game Disc. DVD-R media burn content from the inside (the slowest part of the disc) outward. For Xbox Game Discs, the Game Disc Layout Tool pushes all game content toward the fastest portion of the disc. Even if you were able to pad out your DVD-R media to place game data in the faster part of the disc, it still would not be an Xbox Game Disc, because the volume descriptor, file system, and other important data cannot be placed in the same locations as on the Xbox-specific media. Only the DVD emulator can give you real-world performance behavior.

  • Consider placing less bandwidth-intensive data away from the outer edge. While your first impulse might be to load all data at the outer tracks, in some cases it may not be necessary. As mentioned above, on the outer edge of an Xbox Game Disc, the system has less time to try to compensate for any defects. Placing data 20 percent from the outside edge gives the system 20 percent more time to deal with issues. A good example of when to use this strategy might be when streaming an FMV from the DVD. Given an encoding rate of 2–3 Mb/sec, you could easily place the FMV anywhere on the disc without impacting your ability to feed the decoder.

 

TCR Ramifications

 

Since several of the TCR requirements have fairly specific performance requirements based on disc throughput, this section details how those requirements are affected by media issues. For complete details on certification requirements see the Xbox Guide.

 

  • *2-9 Dynamic Update of Memory Units. Games will not fail certification if media defects cause the dynamic update of memory units to take more than one second.

  • *2-12 Audio Fidelity. Games will not fail certification if audio glitches are caused by media defects on the game disc.

  • *2-15 Game Stability. Games are still required to meet this requirement, even in the presence of media defects on the game disc. Note, however, that non-catastrophic pauses of a short duration related to these defects will not cause the game to fail.

  • *2-20 Error Handling. Games are still required to meet this requirement. The game should not bubble-up media defect errors to the gamer, if game play can be continued.

  • *3-30 Initial Interactive State. A game will not fail certification if a media defect causes the game to take longer than 20 seconds to reach the initial interactive screen.

  • *3-31 Non-Interactive Pause. A game will not fail certification if a media defect causes the game to pause for more than five seconds. The game must respond within a reasonable period of time, however, to ensure that the gamer does not perceive the title as hung or crashed.

  • *3-38 Attract Mode Cycle. A game will not fail certification if a media defect causes the game to remain at the initial interactive screen for more than 75 seconds. The game must still be capable of entering attract mode.
