Mixing Audio for Xbox

By Scott Selfon, Audio Content Consultant, Content and Design Team, Xbox Advanced Technology Group

Introduction

A general goal for the Xbox® video game system from Microsoft is for titles to use reasonable sound levels so that the user won't constantly have to tweak their home stereo from game to game, or leave the volume too high or too low to properly enjoy a title's audio nuances.

This chapter discusses ways to mix your audio so that it will line up with the sounds built into the Xbox, as well as fairly closely matching the audio levels from some of the user's other possible devices. We'll also discuss mixing in general, and methods for balancing normal audio levels with high levels appropriate for intense moments in the game.

As always, if you have any questions on the topics presented here, or suggestions/requests for future topics, please contact content@xbox.com.

Default Levels on Consumer Devices

Ideally, when a consumer turns on their Xbox, the audio should be fairly similar to other devices connected to their receiver. There are two different values of interest here: the peak volume and the average volume. Peak volume is typically measured in decibels relative to full scale (dBFS), while root mean square (RMS) is a typical measurement for perceived loudness. DVD movies generally peak dialog to around –8 to –10 dBFS, with average RMS dialog levels at –22 to –27 dBFS. Television programming peaks at about –8 dBFS, with average RMS dialog levels at approximately –20 to –24 dBFS (depending on the television station). By comparison, CDs and television commercials are often mixed much higher; advertisements tend to "squash" their audio such that average levels are much louder, as have many commercial music recordings in the past few years.1

Rather than compete with such full-scale volume presentations, we've opted to target DVDs and broadcast television signals for the audio levels to match. In particular, we generally recommend that games target –12 dBFS for the peak volume of dialog output from the Xbox (if dialog is present), with average dialog output levels at –22 to –26 dB RMS. This builds in a fair amount of room for dialog to grow (for instance, yelling), and as we'll see below, the –12 dBFS peak has been adopted as default attenuation setting for playback of audio elements.

Unlike other devices, where the audio is pre-rendered and therefore the peak signals known far ahead of time, this allows for dynamic audio to have plenty of headroom for beefy explosions and crescendoing music, as appropriate. In particular, with the potential for 256 voices to simultaneously sound, this default headroom helps avoid some potential issues with clipping.

Audio Levels "Hard-Coded" on Xbox

Before we discuss default audio behaviors in a title, let's first go over the volumes presented by audio elements built into Xbox.

The startup sound peaks at around –13.7 dBFS, and its average non-weighted RMS level is between –24 and –26 dBFS. Again, the general hope is that the user uses this startup sound as a way to set their speaker volume to be comfortable. When compared to a network television or DVD signal, the startup sound is generally loud but comfortable, though individual user tastes will vary.

The Xbox Dashboard is, in general, mixed to peak around –12 dBFS, with an average non-weighted RMS level of –20 to –27 dBFS (depending on section), on the loud end of what we recommend targeting for dialog. The exception to these levels are CD- and WMA-playback from in the Xbox Dashboard, which are played full scale to conform to signals expected out of a consumer CD player. When CDs are ripped into WMAs, the signals are normalized, leading to audio that might be even slightly louder than the source CD audio tracks. Again, this is in the Xbox Dashboard only (although as we'll discuss below, you could potentially play some audio elements in your game at this level if you wanted to).

Recommended Authoring Levels

One potential area of confusion is the fact that volume can be set by the author and/or by the developer. Let's first talk about content authoring. As we said, we're targeting –12 dBFS for the peak volume of dialog as output from the Xbox, with average RMS dialog output levels at –22 to –26 dBFS. How we achieve this can be broken down into two methods:

  1. Pre-author your content to these levels, and do not apply any attenuation at playback. The disadvantage here is that the Xbox hardware in general cannot "amplify" sounds, so if you decide a sound needs to be louder, you'll have to go back to the source content and re-author a louder version. However, when your scene is completely pre-rendered (for instance, a full motion video cutscene), it's often convenient to do this work up front.
  2. Author your content as "hot" as is reasonable, and apply attenuation at playback. Because this is the more common scenario (particularly for 3D-positioned and dynamically mixed sounds), as we'll discuss in a moment, voices actually default to have 12 dB of attenuation applied. With this in mind, if you authored your dialog to peak at full scale, with average RMS output at –10 to –14 dBFS, the 12 dB of attenuation would land you right at the Xbox target audio level.

Most titles will use some combination of these two methods. Cutscenes in particular can be done using either method-either pre-rendered at –12 dBFS peak and average RMS levels of –22 to –26 dBFS (with no attenuation applied at output) or peaking at up to 0 dBFS with dialog RMS at –10 to –14 dBFS, allowing the hardware to attenuate the soundtrack by 12 dB to land at the "target" audio levels mentioned above.

One other note for content authoring and recording: when measuring RMS levels, you will generally want to use weighted RMS scaling. Non-weighted RMS will often give misleading results, especially when bass content isn't watched carefully.

The Xbox provides the IDirectSound8::GetOutputLevels function for monitoring the current peak and RMS levels for the digital and analog outputs. These levels are displayed in many of the audio XDK samples (from which the code can be placed in your own title), as well as in the Audio Console Application and in the PC-side XACT authoring tool.

Default In-Game Audio Levels

In part to allow for the possibility of numerous sounds to mix without clipping, playback on Xbox using DirectSound defaults to –12 dB attenuation on each voice. Typically, audio content should generally be authored as "hot" (loud) as possible, and attenuation/mixing should be performed during playback, not during authoring, to ensure the highest fidelity. The breakdown of how the –12 dB headroom is achieved:

  • For 2D voices, the headroom defaults to 600 millibels (mB) = 6 dB. This can be adjusted using IDirectSoundBuffer8::SetHeadroom or IDirectSoundStream8::SetHeadroom.
  • For 3D (HRTF) voices, headroom defaults to 0 dB. However, the HRTF processing applies the equivalent of 6-dB attenuation, so 2D and 3D voices are effectively balanced in volume by default to –12 dBFS peak. But this also means that a 3D positioned voice cannot be louder than –6 dBFS peak.
  • Mixbins default to –6 dB (more accurately, 1 bit) of headroom. This can be modified by using IDirectSound8::SetMixbinHeadroom for mixbins (for instance, specific speakers or effects paths) you want to hear more loudly.

When the Xbox Audio Creation Tool is being used, the content creator has more complete control over volume levels than they would from DirectSound.

  • Mixbin headroom is removed (set to 0 bits, or 0 dB).
  • For sounds played onto a 2D or the default sound source, the voice headroom continues to default to 6 dB. The content creator can remove this headroom via the Enable 6 dB gain boost checkbox in a sound's mixer properties.
  • For 3D (HRTF) sound sources, headroom remains as in DirectSound, defaulting to 0 dB but with HRTF processing effectively adding roughly 6 dB of attenuation.

Therefore, do note that the default headroom for sounds in XACT is -6 dB, versus the -12 dB for DirectSound.

Note that due to the different implementations—whether the volume of a sound is adjusted in the process of reading it from memory, or a hardware attenuation is applied by the Xbox MCP—voice headroom (calculated software attenuation) is set in millibels (hundredths of a decibel), while mixbin headroom (hardware attenuation) is set in bits (one bit roughly equals 6 dB).

We already said that dialog should target –12 dBFS peak and –22 to –26 dB RMS. Based on the –12 dB attenuation applied by default, this means that you should author dialog such that peaks are normalized to 0 dBFS, and average levels are between –10 and –14 dB RMS, though more likely closer to –14 dB to avoid too much compression. By comparison, television commercials generally have a range of about 12 dB.

One other note on the topic of audio levels: The fact that voices are attenuated by 12 decibels by default does not mean the output level from the Xbox will peak at –12 dBFS. For instance, two full scale authored waves, both attenuated at playback to –12 dBFS peak, will sum to –6 dBFS peak. Four full scale waves will sum to 0 dBFS peak playback. So with the potential of simultaneously playing 256 voices, be careful to avoid digital clipping.

Modifying Audio Levels: The Libraries

As mentioned above, audio levels are actually manipulated at two stages in the audio implementation process: when authored, and when played back. We discussed above some of the recommendations for authoring levels. In this section, we'll discuss how a developer can programmatically adjust the volume of already authored sounds.

There are several different functions and methods in the Xbox Audio Creation Tool, Microsoft® DirectSound® and Microsoft® DirectMusic® made available for altering the volume of voices and mixbins. We'll discuss them each individually, as well as how they are applied additively.

XACT

As previously discussed, much of XACT's volume control is placed into the hands of the person with the "ears", namely the sound designer or composer. For this reason, a programmer does not typically have control of the volume of specific sounds (beyond the attenuation that 3D positioning applies). That said, there are cases where game state changes require dynamic adjustment of volume levels. Below are the methods for adjusting these volumes.

  • IXACTEngine::SetMasterVolume: The volume of all XACT sounds (or a particular category, as defined by the content creator to group their sounds) can be adjusted.
  • IXACTEngine::SetParameterControl: The composer/sound designer can define runtime parameter control (RPC) curves for specific sounds, where a programmer-specified value change can correspond to changes in volume, pitch, filter, and so on. IXACTEngine::SetParameterControl does not necessarily correspond to volume changes, but seems worth mentioning here as one avenue for dynamic volume control.
  • IXACTSoundSource::SetMixBinVolumes: Identical to IDirectSoundBuffer8::SetMixBinVolumes. Sets the per-mixbin volumes that a sound source sends (to up to eight mixbins). By default, these volumes are 0 dBFS.

DirectSound

  • IDirectSoundBuffer8::SetHeadroom/IDirectSoundStream8::SetHeadroom: As discussed above, the default setting for a 2D buffer is 600 mB, or 6 dB. Headroom is set as a positive value.
  • IDirectSoundBuffer8::SetVolume/IDirectSoundStream8::SetVolume: Functionally equivalent to SetHeadroom, but applied additively (separately) and set as a negative value.
  • IDirectSoundBuffer8::SetMixBinVolumes/IDirectSoundStream8::SetMixBinVolumes: Independently set the volumes a buffer or stream will play on individual mixbins (for instance, for manual panning). Set in millibels, and cumulative with both SetVolume and SetHeadroom.
  • IDirectSound8::SetMixbinHeadroom: Controls the output volume of a mixbin (effectively, every sound mixed into it). Set in bits, with one bit rougly equal to 6 dB of headroom.

Not including attenuation created by 3D positioning, filtering, effects, or DirectMusic, sound volume will be roughly:

Total effective attenuation = volume – headroom – mixbin headroom

DirectMusic

  • MIDI Controllers: MIDI messages, whether originating in authored content (segments) or created by the developer, can be used to control volume:

    • MIDI continuous controllers 7 (volume) and 11 (expression)—global volume on a performance channel
    • MIDI controllers 91 (reverb depth) and 93 (chorus)—mixbin volume for FXSEND_0 and FXSEND_1 mixbins
    • MIDI controller 10 (pan)—relative mixbin volumes for the left and right mixbins for a channel
  • Audiopath::SetVolume and SegmentState::SetVolume—control the volume for an audiopath or a playing segment.2
  • IDirectMusicPerformance8::SetGlobalParam using GUID_PerfMasterVolume—Globally affects all DirectMusic content that uses this performance. Typically there is only one performance in use, so in that case, this API call will affect the volume of all playing content.

Default Channel Assignments

When playing back a non-positioned mono sound, keep in mind that the default behavior is to play mono sounds out of the front pair of speakers. In the case of Dolby® Digital or Dolby® Surround, it may at times be more appropriate to play such mono sounds from the center channel only. However, remember that when compared to stereo sounds (which default to the left and right speakers), mono sounds will be the same volume by default.

Listening to the Mix

To make sure all listeners get an audio experience that balances sound elements appropriately, you should generally audition your mix on an Xbox attached to the following systems:

  1. Dolby Digital (5.1 speaker system)
  2. Stereo
  3. Dolby® ProLogic®/Surround (either 4 speakers [left, right, left surround, right surround with phantom center] or through a 5.1 speaker system)—optional; stereo testing may be sufficient, particularly if you are not using 3D (HRTF) voice processing

If you want to be thorough, you could also listen through a mono system (using the Xbox RF Adapter), though stereo should generally be adequate. A few key items to watch for that may appear as differences between these systems:

  • The LFE channel will only be used by a Dolby Digital system; it will not be played by Dolby Surround and stereo environments.
  • The two surround channels are mixed into a single monaural surround channel and attenuated by 3 dB for Dolby Surround playback.

These differences shouldn't necessarily impact your relative mix volume, but are still good to keep in mind while authoring and auditioning your content.

Other Audio Level Tips

  • Dialog, particularly when it provides instructions for gameplay, should generally take priority in the mix.
  • If your title uses the default effects program (dsstdfx.bin), make sure you updated it with the XTL 1.0 final version of the program, or the version supplied with your latest Xbox System Software release. Some pre-release versions of the program had issues with surround-level volumes compared to front left and right.
  • For dynamic audio systems, make sure you pay attention to possible audio levels based on the intensity of the music/ambience. If a high-volume musical cue has just started playing, you might want to duck it for a character's dialog.3

Wrap-Up

Remember that in general, you'll want to author your content such that its peaks are normalized to 0 dBFS. For dialog, author it such that the average loudness is between –10 and –14 dB RMS, which translates into –22 to –26 dB RMS at playback with the default –12 dB attenuation. This should give you a good reference point for mixing other sounds. If all of your sound levels are too quiet or too loud, you can increase them by altering IDirectSound8::SetMixBinHeadroom and/or IDirectSoundBuffer/Stream::SetHeadroom in DirectSound, or by checking the 'Enable 6 dB gain boost' property in an XACT sound's mixer properties.
